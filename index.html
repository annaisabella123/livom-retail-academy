7 Seconds.
No Second Chance.

Before you learn a single technique — understand why the first moment in the showroom decides everything.

01
The Scenario
Picture this
"A couple walks into your showroom. You smile, approach immediately, and say 'Can I help you?' They say: 'No thanks, just looking.' You never speak to them again. They buy the sofa — from a competitor, two weeks later."

This isn't bad luck. It's a pattern. And it starts in the first 7 seconds — before any product is mentioned, before any price is discussed. The greeting itself triggered the refusal.

02
The 4 Core Principles
01 — Mindset
Host, not Salesperson
A guest in your home feels welcomed, not chased. Every customer who enters your showroom deserves that same feeling — regardless of budget.
02 — Sequence
Emotion before Product
Premium advisors speak less than 40% of the time in the first 5 minutes. The customer leads. You listen, ask, understand — then recommend.
03 — Listening
Less talk, more understanding
Your first job is observation — reading pace, body language, intent. What does this person need to feel safe in this space?
04 — Adaptability
Match their pace
Some customers want immediate guidance. Others need 2 minutes of orientation. You adapt — never impose your own rhythm on theirs.

02
The 4-Step Timeline
0–7s
Perceive — before you speak
Read pace, direction of gaze, body language. Calm eye contact, gentle nod. No movement toward the customer yet. Signal sent: I see you. You're welcome.
Why: Walking toward a customer the moment they enter creates immediate pressure. Stillness signals confidence and respect.
7–20s
Greet — warm, pressure-free
Welcome them without asking a question. The rule: no question in the first 20 seconds.
"Welcome — take all the time you need." · "Good afternoon — lovely to have you here."
20–90s
Give Space — the active pause
Stay visible but not intrusive. The customer orients, explores, touches something. Your job: wait for the natural moment of eye contact — that's their invitation.
The golden rule: when they look up and find you — that's the moment. Not before. Not after.
90s+
Open the Dialogue — the anchor technique
Use what they've already done as your opening. They've been looking at something — anchor your question there.
"You've been looking at this piece for a while — may I ask what's on your mind?"

What is the most common mistake advisors make in the first 20 seconds with a new customer?

Question 2 of 5
A customer enters the showroom and immediately starts looking at a sofa. After 70 seconds, they look up and make brief eye contact with you. What should you do?
A
Wait another 30 seconds to be sure they're ready
B
Approach immediately and describe the sofa's features
C
Approach now — this eye contact is their invitation to engage
D
Call to them from where you are to avoid being intrusive
Correct. Customer-initiated eye contact is the clearest natural signal: "I'm open to contact now." Waiting longer after this moment actually creates awkwardness. Approaching now is both natural and expected.
The moment of voluntary eye contact from the customer is a critical signal — they are opening the door. Think about what that specific body language means.
Question 3 of 5
What is the "anchor technique" for opening a conversation?
A
Complimenting the customer's taste immediately to build rapport
B
Using what the customer has already done (looked at, touched, sat on) as the natural starting point for a question
C
Anchoring the price discussion early to set expectations
D
Starting every conversation with the same scripted opener
Perfect. The anchor makes your question feel natural — not scripted. "You've been looking at this one for a while — what caught your attention?" is far more powerful than any generic opener, because it's about them specifically.
The anchor technique is about using the customer's own behavior as your starting point — making the transition from observation to conversation feel completely organic.
Question 4 of 5
When should you offer a customer a coffee or water?
A
Immediately as they enter — before they start looking around
B
Only when the conversation is nearly finished and a sale seems likely
C
After the first real dialogue has started — once they are open and engaged
D
Never — it takes too much time in a busy showroom
Exactly. Too early = intrusive and transactional. After first real contact, the offer transforms from "a sales move" to "genuine hospitality." The customer's emotional state is the trigger — not the clock.
Think about what makes the drink offer feel natural vs. pushy. It's entirely about timing relative to the relationship — not the time on the clock.
Question 5 of 5
A customer enters and avoids eye contact, immediately gets absorbed in examining a display. What does this most likely signal?
A
They are rude and unlikely to buy
B
They want to explore independently — give space, wait for their signal before approaching
C
They haven't noticed you yet — call out to get their attention
D
You should approach immediately before they leave
Correct. Avoiding eye contact while getting absorbed in a product is actually a strong buying signal — they're genuinely interested. Approaching too early would break their focus and feel intrusive. Patience here is a skill.
Read the body language more carefully — absorption in a product with avoided eye contact is not rudeness. Think about what the customer is actually communicating with this behavior.

02
The Question Funnel — 5 Levels

Every great consultation follows one logic: start wide — get specific — then concrete. The funnel guides you from "how do you live?" to "when should it be delivered?"

Level 01
Opener — Wide
Explore lifestyle and living situation. No products yet.
"How do you use your living space?" · "What's your home like right now?"
Level 02
Situation — Specific
Get concrete about daily use, people, habits.
"How many people sit on the sofa daily?" · "Do you have children or pets?"
Level 03
Motivation — Deep
Uncover emotional drivers and real priorities.
"What matters most to you in furniture?" · "What should the new piece change for you?"
Level 04
Constraints
Budget and dimensions — addressed naturally, not awkwardly.
"Are there room dimensions or a budget we should keep in mind?"
Level 05
Decision — Close
Timeline and urgency — when everything is right.
"When everything fits — when should it be in your home?"
03
The 5 Question Types
TypeWhen to UseShowroom Example
OpenTo open up and explore freely"How do you picture your ideal living room?"
ClosedTo confirm or finalise"Is an L-shape a must for you?"
AlternativeTo guide choices, speed up"More fabric or leather?"
DeepeningUncover emotions and real motives"What would that change for you at home?"
RhetoricalCreate reflection, slow down"May I ask what's held you back so far?"
04
Active Listening — The 3 Signals
Body
Stay turned toward the customer. Nod. Eye contact. Never reach for the next product while they're still speaking.
Language
Paraphrase: "So what you're saying is..." — Never interrupt. Never offer a solution before they finish.
Summary
"So we need a large L-sofa, durable, max €3,500 — did I get that right?" — This one sentence builds more trust than any product pitch.
05
The Needs Profile — 4 Things to Know
Before you recommend any product — know these 4 things
01 — Situation
Who lives there? How do they use the space? Lifestyle, number of people, pets, kids?
02 — Motivation
What drives the purchase? Comfort, aesthetics, durability, change of look — or all of these?
03 — Budget
What range feels comfortable? Bring it up early and naturally — avoiding it creates awkwardness.
04 — Timeline
When does it need to arrive? Renovation, move, occasion — urgency shapes the decision.

"You have two ears and one mouth. Use them in that proportion."

What is the correct order of the Question Funnel?

Budget → Motivation → Situation → Opener → Decision

Question 2 of 5
A customer says: "Durability is the most important thing for me." What's the best follow-up?
A
"Our most durable model is this one over here."
B
"What's your budget for this?"
C
"What does durability mean specifically for your situation — children, pets, daily use?"
D
"How long have you been looking?"
Exactly. "Durability" means something different to everyone. A deepening question unpacks the real meaning behind the word — and shows genuine interest. Going straight to product skips this crucial step.
The customer gave you an important word — "durability." Before you can act on it, you need to understand what it means for them specifically. Which question type does that?
Question 3 of 5
What is paraphrasing and when should you use it?
A
Repeating the customer's exact words back to them, always after each sentence
B
Restating what the customer said in your own words to confirm understanding — especially after they share something important
C
Describing a product's features using the customer's vocabulary
D
Summarising the price range the customer mentioned
Correct. Paraphrasing is active listening in action: "So what you're saying is — you need something that survives two dogs and three kids, is that right?" This one technique builds more trust than any product demonstration.
Paraphrasing is a listening technique — not a repetition tool. Think about what it signals to the customer when you reframe what they said in your own words.
Question 4 of 5
You've just finished your questions. The customer's needs are clear. What is the ONE sentence that should bridge from discovery to product?
A
"Let me show you our bestsellers."
B
"Based on your price range, this one is a good option."
C
"Based on what you've shared — I immediately thought of something that fits exactly. May I show you?"
D
"Do you want to see some options?"
Perfect. This bridge sentence does two things: it signals that you actually listened, and it creates anticipation. The customer feels the recommendation is personal — because it is.
The bridge from needs to product should reference what you just learned. Which option makes the customer feel their answers actually mattered?
Question 5 of 5
When is the right moment to recommend a specific product?
A
As soon as the customer enters and mentions what they're looking for
B
After you can explain in one sentence why this specific piece is right for this specific person
C
After 5 minutes, regardless of what has been discussed
D
Whenever the customer stops talking
Exactly right. If you can't complete the sentence "This piece is right for you because [specific reason based on what they told you]" — you haven't asked enough questions yet.
The timing of a product recommendation should be determined by knowledge, not by time. Think about what you need to know before you can make a truly personal recommendation.

DeepeningTo uncover emotions and motives"What would that change for you at home?"
RhetoricalTo create reflection and slow down"May I ask what's held you back so far?"
04
Active Listening — 3 Signals
Body
Stay turned toward them. Nod. Eye contact. Never reach for the next product while they are still speaking.
Language
Paraphrase: "So what you're saying is..." — Never interrupt. Never offer a solution before they finish.
Summary
"So we need a large L-sofa, durable, max €3,500 — did I get that right?" One sentence. Enormous trust.
05
The Needs Profile — Know These 4 Things
Before you recommend any product
01 Situation
Who lives there? How do they use the space? Lifestyle, people, pets, kids?
02 Motivation
What drives the purchase? Comfort, aesthetics, durability, change of look?
03 Budget
What range feels comfortable? Bring it up early and naturally.
04 Timeline
When does it need to arrive? Renovation, move, occasion — urgency shapes decisions.
06
The Bridge to the Product
The rule: only recommend once you can complete this sentence:
"Based on what you've shared — I immediately thought of something that fits exactly. May I show you?"
